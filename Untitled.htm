ABSTRACT

The burning process of multiple liquid fuels with various nanoenergetic additives is investigated experimentally in the suspended droplet configuration. Specifically, the burning rate constant K and ignition delay Tign are measured for two base fuels, rocket grade kerosene (RP-2) and ethanol, with seven energetic additives; the investigated additives include aluminum (Al), magnesium oxide (MgO), graphene, graphene nano-platelet (GNP), as well as numerous soluble additives including ammonia borane (AB), ammonium nitrate (NaAN) and sodium borohy-dride (NaBH4),all ofwhich do not exceed 6 wt.% loading concentration. Results from these experiments show that in general, there is not a significant modification to the steady burning rate for these nanofuel combinations (averaging < 10%) for the loading concentrations considered. Instead, the additives exhibit a majority of particle burning during the end of the droplet lifetime, which generally are short-lived events that do not affect the overall K. This is illustrated by calculating the instantaneous burning rate K for these nanofuels, where K is 2% to 18% higher than pure fuels during the last half of the droplet burn time depending on the additive type. Similarly, nanofuel ignition delays generally only exhibit modest changes for a majority of the investigated combinations, causing only a 士 5% change on average. However, two nanofuels consisting of ethanol with 5 wt.% soluble ammonia borane and RP-2 with 1 wt.% suspended graphene do exhibit increases in Tign of approximately 20 and 30%, respectively. These nanofuel compounds also exhibit substantial qualitative combustion behavioral changes including flame color changes and micro-droplet shedding events for ethanol with ammonia borane, as well as a notable reduction in the average burning rate of 20% for RP-2 with graphene. Therefore, these two nanofuel combinations may be candidates for further study as a means to garner control of combustion dynamics in a reacting liquid rocket spray environment.

Introduction

Heterogeneous mixtures incorporating a pure liquid base fuel with metallic fuel additives have been a topic of interest in propulsion applications dating back to the 1940s, primarily due to the potential for significantly increased energy densities and overall performance increases. The earliest known study of heterogeneous mixtures for rocket applications was performed by Heinz Mueller’s group of Bavarian Motor Works (BMW) (Clark 2018),

Unfortunately, these engines suffered from very low performance due to incomplete additive burning, yielding overall chamber pressures lower than 1/3 of the desired level (Clark 2018). However, more recently, this research area has garnered renewed interest through the use of metalized nanoparticles (i.e., particles less than 1 “m) as the suspended additives. Essentially, nanoparticles (NP’s) consist of threedimensional clusters or two-dimensional sheets of atomic and molecular structure of inorganic materials that are nanometer size, typically 1 to 100 nm or even 1 to 1000 nm. Overall, nanoparticles offer a significantly large surface area to volume ratio compared to particles on the micron scale and above, which theoretically correspond to much higher reactivity. This interest resurgence is mostly a direct result of substantial successes of nanoparticle utilization in the fields of combustion science and nanofluids (Saidur, Leong, and Mohammad 2011; Yetter, Risha, and Son 2009), which have demonstrated improvements in performance of explosives and solid propellants through the addition of various nanostructured energetic materials such as micron and sub-micron size aluminum additives to polymer mixtures (Armstrong et al. 2003).

Expanding upon the research started by BMW during the 1940s, the automotive industry has since adopted NP additives for hydrocarbon fuels (Tyagi et al. 2008), with an interest in their catalytic effects for the reduction of tail pipe gas emission (Basha and Gopal 2012). There are wide ranges of elemental and compound NP’s, as well as metallic alloys that are desirable liquid fuel additives due to their energetic properties, reactivity and/or catalytic effects (Ghamari and Ratner 2017; Jones et al. 2011; Sabourin et al. 2009; Sabourin, Yetter, and Parimi 2010). Research into biodiesel nanoadditives has successfully demonstrated the use of manganese oxide (MnO), cobalt oxide, and cerium oxide functionalized carbon nanotubes in various nanobiodesel blends to reduce carbon monoxide (CO) emissions through an oxidation catalysis process (Mehregan and Moghiman 2018; Mirzajanzadeh et al. 2015). However, it should be noted that while there is evidence to support nanoadditives as a means to reduce harmful emissions, certain researchers did not observe any significant difference between the amount of CO emission for neat biodiesel and blends with nanoparticles (Soudagar et al. 2018).

For propulsion applications, fundamental droplet combustion studies with nanoener-getic additives as burning rate modifiers have been investigated by numerous research groups using a variety of liquid base fuels and additive combinations (Ghamari and Ratner 2017; Guerieri, DeLisio, and Zachariah 2017; Javed, Baek, and Waheed 2015; Pfeil et al. 2010; Pfeil et al. 2013; Sim et al. 2018). In these experiments, varying levels of burning rate modification were observed, including increases in differing amounts from small variations (Pfeil et al. 2010; Pfeil et al. 2013), to moderate (Sim et al. 2018), or even a decrease (Guerieri, DeLisio, and Zachariah 2017), depending on the additive type (i.e., solid or soluble) and base fuel. In general, results from these studies indicate that the augmentation of burning rates is possible up to approximately 15% for < 5 wt.% additive concentration, but it is typically no greater than 10%. Effects on nanofuel droplet ignition delay Tign have also been reported (Javed, Baek, and Waheed 2015; Kim, Baek, and Yoon 2016), which also show either an increase (Kim, Baek, and Yoon 2016) or decrease (Javed, Baek, and Waheed 2015; Kim, Baek, and Yoon 2016) depending on the nanofuel combination and ambient conditions, i.e., temperature and pressure, of the surrounding environment.

Aside from overall performance benefits, there is interest in using nanostructured fuel additives to control combustion dynamics in liquid rocket engines (LRE’s). As detrimental thermo-acoustic instabilities are excited due to in-phase coupling between oscillatory chamber pressure and unsteady heat release from combustion, it is theorized that nanoe-nergetic additives can affect this coupling to potentially lead to a damping effect. One experimental study investigating the addition of a soluble additive ammonia borane (AB), NH3BH3, into ethanol was studied in a model LRE combustor (Pfeil, Son, and Anderson 2014). Compared to pure ethanol, it was found that there existed an axial bimodal heat release distribution throughout the combustor present with ethanol and AB at 6 wt.%. While this spatial relocation of the heat release did alter flame-acoustic coupling within the chamber, unfortunately, the ethanol with ammonia borane nanofuel actually caused the engine to become more unstable across various injector configurations than operating with neat ethanol (Pfeil, Son, and Anderson 2014). Nevertheless, this demonstrated the potential for significantly altering the combustion dynamic processes within the combustor during operation.

Another experimental study investigating the flame-acoustic coupling of a nanofuel was recently investigated in the continuously-fed burning droplet geometry (Sim et al. 2019). The investigated nanofuel mixture consisted of ethanol and the non-soluble additive nanoaluminum (nAl) up to 3 wt.% loading. Overall, it was found that nAl addition increased the degree of flame-acoustic coupling (i.e., Rayleigh index G) in the presence of a velocity antinode up to 45% on average during sustained oscillatory combustion. Also, the mean strain rate attributed to periodic partial extinction (PPER) phenomena (Bennewitz et al. 2018) that occurs at higher amplitude forcing was shown to increase up to 44% with the addition of nAl, as well as delaying the onset of PPER for lower frequency forcing at 332 Hz (Sim et al. 2019). This further indicates the possibility of altering the flame-acoustic coupling with the use of energetic nanoparticles.

In order to lay the foundation for developing a nanofuel that can garner control of combustion dynamics, substantial background work in understanding the effects of nanostructured materials and nano-energetics as rocket relevant liquid fuel additives is first required. One fundamental approach is to study the combustion characteristics of suspended nanofuel droplets, as this provides a suitable means to investigate condensed phase combustion (Godsave 1953; Law and Williams 1972; Sirignano 2010). Therefore, the focus of this experimental study is to investigate the effect of various nanoadditives using two rocket relevant base fuels in the suspended droplet geometry to discern changes in the burning rate constant K and ignition delay time 侦.

The emphasis in this study is on relatively low loading of nanoenergetics (< 1 wt.% for solids, < 6 wt.% for solubles), in consideration of the fairly low tolerance many engine systems have for elevated particulate levels, due to concerns over particulate accumulation leading to potential clogging or failure of engine components. For example, the military specification for rocket grade kerosene only permits a maximum of 1 mg/L of solid particulates for delivered fuel, or only about 10-4 wt.% (USAF 2006). Thus, even the relatively low loading levels considered in this paper are still orders of magnitude larger than the current specification. The implementation of even these loadings would therefore as a minimum requires a reexamination of current fuel specifications, and beyond that it could also require a substantial redesign of engine system components.

The investigated base fuels in this study include ethanol and rocket grade kerosene (RP-2), with solid nanoadditives including aluminum (Al), magnesium oxide (MgO), graphene, graphene nano-platelet (GNP), and the soluble additives ammonia borane, ammonium nitrate (NaAN) and sodium borohydride (NaBHq). The three main solid additives aluminum, graphene nano-platelet, and magnesium oxide mainly target modifying transport and thermodynamic fuel properties in various ways. Specifically, aluminum has shown to increase overall heat release of the reacting system through the increase of the flame temperature (Sim et al. 2018; Tanvir and Qiao 2015) and heat of combustion (Sim et al. 2018). Additionally, the increased thermal conductivity of aluminum-based nanofuels (Pfeil et al. 2010; Sim et al. 2018; Tanvir and Qiao 2015) has also shown a benefit to droplet burning. Graphene nano-platelet has similarly demonstrated an increase in internal heat transfer within the liquid droplet due to increased thermal conductivity (Ghamari and Ratner 2017), as well as increased heat absorption through radiation due to the darkened nature of GNP (Ghamari and Ratner 2017). Magnesium oxide has previously exhibited the ability to provide a quality dispersion in nonpolar fuels (Bello et al. 2015), which makes it desirable from a flow system infrastructure and storability standpoint for a reactive system. In addition, MgO has shown increased diffusive behavior in kerosene fuels, leading to similar diffusive timescales compared to aluminum and much shorter radiative timescales (Bello et al. 2015).

Regarding the soluble additives, ammonia borane is unique in that it provides a mechanism for internal hydrogen gas generation to occur during droplet burning, as the dissociation temperature for ammonia borane is lower than the boiling point of its base fuel (ethanol) (Pfeil et al. 2013, 2014). This hydrogen gas generation causes an increase in the chemical kinetic rates (Pfeil et al. 2013), and can potentially help mitigate the onset of combustion instabilities in a reactive system (Yu et al. 2008). Ammonia borane addition has also shown to increase overall heat release through increased flame temperature, heat of combustion and thermal conductivity (Pfeil et al. 2013, 2014). In total, 10 nanofuel combinations are studied in the present work for loading concentrations under 6 wt.%. Generally, most nanofuel combinations at these loading concentrations do not exhibit large changes in global droplet burning properties but show an increase in the instantaneous burning rate toward the end of the droplet lifetime. Both ethanol with 5 wt.% AB and RP-2 with 1 wt.% graphene demonstrate the largest changes to droplet burning, permitting them to be suitable for further investigation in a reacting liquid nanofuel spray.

Nanofuel creation

Synthesis and dispersion of nanoparticles is an expansive topic and full treatment is beyond the scope of this article. Synthesis and application of NP’s have been the subject of great interest to researchers in a wide range of disciplines including physics, chemistry, material science, engineering, biology, and medicine. Several comprehensive and specialized review articles on the synthesis and applications of functional NP’s have appeared in recent publications (Lu, Salabas, and Schuth 2007; Mittal, Chisti, and Banerjee 2013； Tang, Li, and Chen 2012). However, the focus in the following two sections will be limited to the aspects of synthesis and dispersion of nanoparticles that are directly related to liquid nanofuels.

Synthesis of nanoparticles

There are two major approaches regarding the synthesis of NP’s and preparation of nanosuspensions, designated as either top-down or bottom-up nanofabrication (Biswas et al. 2012). Top-down nanofabrication involves starting from a larger piece of material and etching away to produce nanoparticles, whereas the bottom-up fabrication process builds nanostructures from subcomponents on the molecular level through an additive procedure (Biswas et al. 2012). One issue encountered in the synthesis of elemental NP’s or metal alloys is the formation of oxide layers immediately after the NP’s have been formed or soon after they are exposed to environmental oxygen and moisture (Meziani et al. 2009). For example, in the case of aluminum NP’s, the relatively thick AbO3 layer/shell is undesirable for three reasons: (i) the surface layer ofNP’s is a very stable oxide unlike the highly reactive Al, (ii) it is an impenetrable shell with a very high melting temperature, far higher than that of Al and (iii) for smaller NP’s (e.g., less than 80 nm), a relatively large percentage of the available material (depending on the size) is nonenergetic (i.e., significantly reducing the large surface area to volume ratio).

The use of transmission electron microscopy (TEM) is crucial in revealing structural details of the solid nanoparticles prior to dispersion into the liquid fuel. This illustrates the average particle size and distribution compared to the manufacturer specified size. In addition, TEM images usually reveal how densely the clusters are packed and if there exists substantial porosity within the structures, assuming the drying process does not significantly affect porosity. Overall, TEM encompasses a quality assessment of the raw nanoparticles. Example TEM images for 80 nm aluminum (see Figure 1) taken from two separate batches from the same vendor demonstrate large discrepancies in the nanostructures. To generate these images, sonication of the samples containing ethanol with aluminum nanoparticles is first performed for 10 min, after which the nanofuel mixture is exposed to atmospheric conditions to permit all liquid to evaporate. In these images, it is apparent that batch #1 (Figure 1(a)) contains uniformly spherical particles that are below 100 nm and on average approximate the specified 80 nm size. Batch #2 however, has severely different nanostructures that include large irregularities in both shape and porosity (Figure 1(b)). Nanostructure aggregates largely exceeding 100 nm are present, often with serrated structural boundaries in lieu of spherically smooth edges. As batch #2 contains particles of such poor quality, only aluminum NP’s from batch #1 are used for testing in the current study. However, this illustrates the importance of performing TEM on all purchased raw nanoparticles to ensure their quality.

TEM views of two other types of nanoparticles including 20 nm magnesium oxide and 5 to 7 “m graphene nano-platelet display different structural characteristics to aluminum (see Figure 2). Magnesium oxide shows a combination of both spherical and elliptical shaped particles, which on average appear to be 20 nm in size (Figure 2(a)). This is shown in greater detail in the zoomed view on the right, which displays nanoparticles that are more ovular and have less defined edges than the spherical nAl particles. Graphene nanoplatelet (Figure 2(b)) are nanostructures comprised in a multi-layered sheet geometry that is approximately 5 to 7 “m in size with under 30 layers per structure. Unlike the other particle types, GNP has sharp-edge boundaries that have well-defined corners throughout the nanoclusters. These three main solid particle types are selected as the droplet burning modifiers due to their drastically differing size and structure.

Dispersion of nanoparticles within liquid fuel

Dispersion and proper suspension of solid nanoparticles within a liquid base fuel are crucial to nanofuel performance. This involves creating a well-dispersed suspension of NP’s in the fuel of choice, which for this study is either an alcohol, i.e., ethanol, or a complex hydrocarbon, i.e., rocket grade kerosene (RP-2). Both the base fuel and nanoadditive type affect the strategy to adequately disperse and suspend the solid particles. Most often, a surfactant is employed to reduce the surface tension and make NP’s disperse in the liquid more effectively, in effect creating a nano-suspension through the formation of nanoclusters that stays suspended for a longer period. Much of the earlier work in this area has been performed on the suspension of poorly soluble drugs for drug delivery (Agrawal and Patel 2011; Chingunpituk 2007). Surfactants including a sugar-based type called Span 80, oleic acid (OA), as well as the sodium-based sodium bis(2-ethylhexyl) sulfosuccinate (NaAOT) all have shown to be effective at nanoparticle suspension in nonpolar fuels (Bello et al. 2015; Ghamari and Ratner 2017; Javed, Baek, and Waheed 2015). Aside from the use of surfactants, in certain cases it is necessary to perform surface treatment on the NP’s such as passivation to counter-balance the tendency of NP’s to aggregate and form a much larger cluster (Guerieri et al. 2015).

The aggregation process in a suspension is generally very slow but severely reduces the reactivity of NP’s by removing the most important characteristic property of NP’s, i.e., the extremely large surface area to volume ratio (Deng et al. 2016). Specifically, this is what permits NP’s to have extremely high reactivity, advantageous catalytic properties, as well as affect liquid phase properties such as increased thermal conductivity. The only exception concerning the loss of such desirable properties is when the NP aggregates are extremely porous, which is rare and should not be assumed without due investigation.

However, highly porous materials with nano-scale pores have shown high-reactivity and catalytic properties that makes them behave more like NP’s, unlike other mesoscale materials (Tang, Li, and Chen 2012).

The intricacies of NP dispersions in liquid fuels for propulsion applications have not received significant attention up until now, due to the lack of details about nanodispersion in published reports. In fact, most NP’s show a tendency to form sizable aggregates when introduced to most liquids. It is quite possible that NP’s supplied by vendors as powder are mostly in large clusters well exceeding 500 nm, which is the reason the sonication process is required to properly break down the NP clusters within a liquid. Typically, the use of a high-powered sonication probe/tip is recommended instead of an ultrasonic bath, which may only be marginally effective in some cases. It should be noted that even with ultrasonication using a probe, it may not be able to break apart a strongly bonded cluster of NP’s.

Typically, the formed NP aggregates are relatively large in size and can grow up to a few micrometers (Anton, Benoit, and Saulnier 2008), making it difficult to achieve a stable nano-dispersion (Hwang et al. 2008). Such aggregates may stay suspended within the liquid long enough so that the suspension passes a standard turbidity test, which denotes how fast solid particles settle at the bottom of a container. In addition, the quality of the NP’s dispersion in fuels is most often solely judged by the turbidity of the liquid. Such a test only reveals the extreme cases of suspension settling and it cannot be used as an indication for the quality of a nano-suspension or any form of well-suspended NP’s in the liquid. For most nanofuels that pass the turbidity test, although the loading concentration of the added NP’s is high enough, it is possible that most NP’s are contained in larger clusters, causing the concentrations of NP’s and nanoclusters to be much less than what is required to cause a noticeable NP-related effect. Agglomeration effects such as these have been noted as a reason behind the variation in the droplet burning rates observed among various research groups using the same additive (Guerieri, DeLisio, and Zachariah 2017).

Proper nano-dispersion is achieved when a majority of the suspended NP’s are in relatively small clusters, with each structure containing no more than several hundred NP’s (Hwang et al. 2008). For example, nanofuels used in this study with nominally 80 nm Al NP’s are shown to have a reasonably good dispersion when most of the nano-clusters are smaller than approximately 500 nm, as revealed by particle size distribution measurements using a zetasizer instrument taken long after the NP’s were added to the fuel and subsequent sonication. For RP-2, a good dispersion is only achieved with the inclusion of a surfactant, typically < 5 wt.% with the NP’s.

An effective approach to determine a reasonably good dispersion of NP’s in a liquid is through a series of periodic measurements of particle size distribution to obtain temporal evolution of the size distribution of nanoparticle clusters in the liquid. However, this approach proved to be ineffective with additives of very large aspect ratio, as with graphene nano-platelet. For GNP, the optical detection system of the zetasizer instrument consistently detects the larger platelet dimensions on the micron scale while leaving the smaller features on the nanometer scale obscured, not permitting an accurate account of the total particle size.

While TEM is helpful in learning aspects of the dispersion quality of NP’s in liquid, it has its own limitations. The main limitation stems from the fact that typical TEM measurements are performed on dry samples and the drying process usually introduces aggregation of NP’s, as well as occasional impurities. A better approach is to perform cryogenic TEM imaging, which involves observation of a matrix of NP clusters within the frozen base fuel, near liquid nitrogen temperature. However, this type of TEM, which is commonly used for biological samples, is costly and may not be available to most researchers. Nevertheless, it is important to characterize the quality of both the raw nanoparticles and their suspension within the liquid fuel prior to experimentation.

Experiment setup

The test facility used to investigate the burning characteristics of various nanofuel droplets is the same as our previous works (Badakhshan, Bennewitz, and Talley 2018; Bennewitz, Badakhshan, and Talley 2019). It allows suspended droplets to be automatically ignited and imaged throughout their entire burn time (see Figure 3). For each experiment, a droplet is suspended from an uncoated quartz fiber from Polymicro Technologies, Inc. that is 150 “m in diameter; the formation of a droplet on the fiber is greatly facilitated by producing a “bead” at the fiber end, which is achieved through melting the tip by a high-temperature flame using an oxy-propane torch. It should be noted that no appreciable change in the surface tension of the hydrocarbon fuel is observed with any of the surfactants in this study, i.e., Span 80, oleic acid, and NaAOT; this is indicated by the droplets of the same initial size staying suspended on the supporting fiber. The fiber bead is nominally 200 “m and the diameter of a typical nanofuel droplet range between d = 1.2 to 1.5 mm. A hypodermic needle capable of repeatably dispensing the same amount of fluid is used to transfer the droplet onto the fiber as a means to generate droplets of similar initial size.

Direct high-speed visible imaging of the burning droplet is performed using a Phantom v7.1 camera with a Nikon AF Micro Nikkor 200 mm lens. Images are recorded at a rate of 1000 fps for the droplet lifetime (typically 2 s), which are backlit using a 2 x 2 square array

Droplet burning characteristics

As with our previous droplet work (Bennewitz, Badakhshan, and Talley 2019), the two burning properties investigated in this study are the burning rate constant K and ignition delay «. These two parameters have become the standard in experimental droplet studies for both singlecomponent (Bartle et al. 2011; Bennewitz et al. 2018; Godsave 1953; Kumagai and Isoda 1955; Law and Williams 1972; Sioui and Roblee 1969) and multicomponent pure fuels (Bennewitz et al. 2018; Ghamari and Ratner2017; Godsave 1953; Pfeil et al. 2010; Wood and Wise 1957), as well as various nanofuels (Ghamari and Ratner 2017; Javed, Baek, and Waheed 2015; Kim, Baek, and Yoon 2016; Pfeil et al. 2010; Pfeil et al. 2013; Sim et al. 2018); they provide quantifiable measures of both the quasi-steady burning and ignition processes, which offer insight into underlying mechanisms that can affect flame-acoustic coupling within an LRE combustor.

In order to determine the burning rate constant, an automated image processing technique is implemented to track the droplet d-squared temporal regression; this takes the high-speed visible backlit images starting 200 ms after ignition and fits an ellipse to the droplet’s outer edge in each image. By processing synthetic image data with a known linear regression using this automated procedure, this approach has been previously shown to be sufficiently robust as it provides less than 1.5% deviation on average (Bennewitz, Badakhshan, and Talley 2019). Using the droplet diameter temporal history, the burning rate can then be evaluated.

As the d-squared law is an idealization describing steady droplet burning, in reality, certain phenomena including droplet heating, multicomponent distillation effects, and vapor accumulation (Law 1982; Law and Williams 1972) can cause deviations from linear d-squared droplet regression behavior. Nevertheless, reporting “effective^ droplet burning rates is useful in describing steady burning processes. To provide K values that are the most direct implementation of the linear regression idea, our previously developed K evaluation method (Bennewitz, Badakhshan, and Talley 2019) is implemented. This approach automatically determines the burning rate based upon the most linear portion of the d-squared regression for the longest period of droplet burning. This approach has successfully shown to provide K values with small statistical and total uncertainties (Bennewitz, Badakhshan, and Talley 2019) for a large range of hydrocarbon fuels exhibiting varying degrees of nonlinear regression behavior. Therefore, this evaluation method is adopted for the current study.

A final consideration for nanofuel droplet combustion is end-of-life processes that can contain a notable amount of additive burning and particle expulsion events. For the traditional solid nanofuels investigated in this work, a majority of the particle burning events take place as essentially “char” combustion, where there is a significantly higher amount of solid additives left compared to the liquid base fuel. In order to be consistent with the burning droplet theory, reporting burning rates as defined by the d-squared law is only appropriate when there is a perfectly spherical or ellipsoidal droplet that is not significantly distorted. Therefore, the image processing approach implemented here addresses this by only considering the droplet regression data until there ceases to be a reduction in the liquid droplet size, as when there are predominantly only solids present, different physical mechanisms begin to dominate and cause the d-squared law to no longer apply. Once there is no liquid fuel left, i.e., denoted by a constant diameter output from the droplet tracking algorithm, all data past this point is removed to report the burning rates.

Ignition delay

Ignition delay time Tign is the other parameter used in this study to quantify droplet burning behavior that may influence combustion dynamics within a reacting spray. Typically, Tign is defined as the time separation between introducing the droplet to a hot ignition environment and the observation of the established flame, which denotes the start of quasi-steady droplet burning (Aggarwal 2014). Previously, many droplet ignition experiments have used arc furnace heating or an ignition coil (i.e., nichrome wire) for combustion initiation, which is generally slow acting. Also, on occasion a flame ignitor may be used, commonly in a miniature torch or small flat flame arrangement. Nevertheless, the differences between various ignition sources primarily can be attributed to two main parameters, (i) the temperature of the source inducing auto-ignition of the droplet and (ii) amount of energy exchange and speed of the heat exchange mechanisms between the ignition source and the fuel droplet, as it influences the rate of vaporization. Other parameters that affect ignition include the availability and mixing of the gaseous oxidizer, proximity of the ignition source to the droplet, and the geometrical spread of the heating source.

The short-duration plasma arc provides a convenient method to systematically study droplet ignition behavior due to its constant average power and adjustable ignition duration, while simultaneously producing minimal background light and heat detected by infrared imaging. The gas temperature for PAI is estimated to exceed 3000 K and the delivered power is at a rate of 2 W or 0.2 J per ignition event of 100 ms duration (Badakhshan, Bennewitz, and Talley 2018). In addition, the short-duration ignition is only active during a fraction of the total droplet lifetime, which isolates quasi-steady droplet burning from the ignition process. As PAI provides repeatable ignition events for all investigated nanofuels, it is used in this study for the ignition experiments.

Ignition delay values are evaluated using the same approach developed in our previous work (Bennewitz, Badakhshan, and Talley 2019). This method automatically tracks the flame region growth from the IR images throughout the ignition process to the start of quasi-steady burning. Instantaneous burning rate

As nanoadditive chemical reaction tends to occur during the end of droplet burning, the instantaneous burning rate K is calculated throughout the droplet lifetime to determine if the nanoadditives affect end-of-life burning substantially. K is determined by taking the d-squared regression temporal history and calculating the numerical derivative using the procedure outlined in the work of Chartrand (Chartrand 2011). This approach is suited to determine the numerical derivative of noisy data in situations where traditional finite difference schemes provide an unacceptable level of noise; it is based on total variation regularization, which permits a reduction in the noise amplification inherent to the numerical derivative calculation. The total variation regularization is essentially a penalty that accounts for the total deviation of the input data, and the regularization parameter a provides weighting of this penalty in the minimizer function for the numerical derivative calculation (Chartrand 2011). Essentially, a is a smoothing filter for the calculated numerical derivative, balancing the noise and solution accuracy. A lower level of a will capture the bulk mean solution, whereas a higher a contains a larger amount of fluctuations within the solution. As suggested by Chartrand, a sensitivity analysis is performed for varying levels of a from 10-1 to 10_5. Results from this analysis for a pure ethanol droplet show that a = 10-1 provides a continuously smooth temporal history of K based on the d-squared droplet regression (see Figure 11(a)), essentially capturing the bulk solution. To ensure the accuracy of the approach, the d-squared droplet regression data is reconstructed through taking the antidifferentiated numerical derivative of K and then comparing it to experimental data. As shown in Figure 11(b), the reconstructed d-squared regression data follows the actual experimental data extremely closely, which yields only an average of 0.52% percent error. Ignition delay

Ignition delays for six nanofuel combinations are measured by taking an average of five identical ignition events and post-processing the IR images using the procedure described in our previous work (Bennewitz, Badakhshan, and Talley 2019). All of the nanofuel ignition delays are normalized using the neat fuel baseline Tign;o for comparison (see Table 1). Ignition delays defined using this approach are found to correlate closely with liquid boiling temperature for the previously investigated pure hydrocarbon fuels (Bennewitz, Badakhshan, and Talley 2019), essentially providing validation to this experimental method. Four nanofuel combinations with ethanol are considered, with two additional mixtures using RP-2. In general, ignition delays for the solid additives in ethanol and RP-2 remain within 土 5% of the pure fuel (see Figure 14). This is expected as these additives do not show any evidence of particle reaction during the onset of droplet combustion, but rather at the end of droplet burning. However, it is notable that the soluble nanofuel ammonia borane in ethanol exhibits a fairly large increase in ignition delay approximating 20%. This is consistent with the location of maximum unsteady heat release for ethanol with ammonia borane in the work ofPfeil et al. (M. A. Pfeil, Son, and Anderson 2014) being pushed downstream in their laboratory scale rocket combustor. Aside from this, RP-2 with 1 wt.% graphene exhibits the largest increase in Tign approaching 30%.

Thermoacoustic instabilities are typically associated with a feedback cycle among oscillatory velocity u0, pressure p0 and heat release qf with the combustor of a reactive system. Growth of the instability occurs when the maximum amplitude oscillatory pressure and heat release are in-phase with one another both spatially and temporally, as per the well-known Rayleigh criterion (Rayleigh 1878). To dampen an instability, designed suppression techniques generally attempt to either reduce the amplitude of the oscillatory pressure or heat release, or decouple p0 and q0 (Harrje 1972). Increasing the ignition delay time for the reacting system could potentially cause the location of maximum heat release to be spatially shifted downstream, away from the pressure anti-node (maximum p0 amplitude) that is inherently located at the injection head. This can lead to a decoupling of the maximum p0 and q0 to help mitigate the onset of a thermoacoustic instability. Concluding remarks

Droplet combustion parameters for a variety of nanofuel combinations are investigated experimentally in this study. The emphasis in this study is on relatively low loadings of nanoenergetics in consideration of the fairly low tolerance many engine systems have for elevated particulate levels. Two rocket relevant base fuels RP-2 and ethanol are considered, with multiple solid nanoadditives including aluminum (Al), magnesium oxide (MgO), graphene, graphene nano-platelet (GNP), as well as the soluble additives ammonia borane (AB), ammonium nitrate (NaAN) and sodium borohydride (NaBHq). Two series of experiments are performed for these nanofuels, aimed to separately measure the burning rate constant K and ignition delay 殃.Based on the effective burning rate results, it is difficult to conclude that low concentration (< 6 wt.%) of most energetic solids has a significant effect on the steady-state burning characteristics of nanofuels. A similar conclusion can be made for a majority of the traditional solid nanoadditives regarding ignition delay, which generally only causes a 土 5% change. However, instantaneous burning rates on average do show an increase above average K values at the end of the droplet lifetime during times where additive reaction typically occurs. Also, the ignition delay for ethanol with 5 wt.% ammonia borane, as well as RP-2 with 1 wt.% graphene is lengthened 20 to 30% compared to pure fuel, indicating they may be capable of altering flame-acoustic coupling. Therefore, these two nanofuel combinations may be considered for investigation in a reactive liquid nanofuel spray. Overall, the results from this study show that a majority of traditional solid additive nanofuels cause only subtle changes to the global droplet burning parameters for the additive concentration levels relevant for propulsion applications, making it challenging to easily identify promising nanofuel candidates for future liquid propulsion studies.

Experiments were conducted to examine the combustion characteristics of refined, bleached and deodorized soybean oil and butanol blends. Single droplets were suspended on microfibers in a combustion chamber at atmospheric pressure and ignited with a hot wire. Ignition characteristics and burning behaviors including burning to completion, burning with microexplosion and incomplete combustion were analyzed for initial concentrations ranging from 25-75% butanol. Droplet size and temperature measurements were analyzed throughout the droplet lifetimes. Relative concentrations prior and during combustion were estimated. Temperature measurements at ignition and during combustion were analyzed. The addition ofbutanol significantly lowered the ignition temperature. All mixtures studied ignited similarly to pure butanol droplets. The results were consistent with closed-cup flashpoint temperatures of butanol-soybean oil blends. A three-staged burn including a microexplosion was observed for all mixed droplets. Burning characteristics suggest a diffusion limited gasification mechanism, which has been previously linked to bi-component droplets with high volatility differentials. Microexplosions occur as a result of the more volatile component trapped within the droplet superheating at flame shrinkage. As butanol decreased to near equi-volume concentrations the microexplosions occurred earlier in the combustion process leading to higher concentrations of butanol trapped within the droplet at flame shrinkage. Therefore, equi-volume mixtures exhibited microexplosions with the greatest intensity. Blends of near equal concentrations by volume proved to exhibit the most favorable combustion characteristics. Bu40 exhibited the most violent microexplosions of all mixtures studied.

Carbon dioxide produced from fossil fuel combustion is a major contributor of anthropogenic climate change. Volatility in petroleum prices and dwindling resource supply necessitate study of efficient methods of alternative fuel utilization. Biomass derived oils (BDOs) are an attractive alternative fuel option. They are domestically grown thus benefiting local economies, have comparatively short carbon life cycles and the infrastructure is in place for

Biodiesel is produced through transesterification of soybean oil. Transesterification improves combustion characteristics by lowering viscosity and flashpoint through conversion of triglycerides to methyl esters. Production and utilization of ethanol and biodiesel has been largely dependent on tax incentives and government mandates. Currently many states have biodiesel and ethanol mandates for required blending with diesel and gasoline, respectively. However, several disadvantages exist with these fuel options. Ethanol, primarily produced from corn has had deleterious impacts on food prices, while the transesterification process comprises 60% of the total energy required to bring biodiesel to market. A recent study of the energy life cycle of soybean-based biodiesel performed by Pradhan et al. for the United States Department of Agriculture found that the fossil energy ratio of biodiesel is 4.56 [1]. Every portion of the biodiesel production process requires an energy input, from the presses required to extract oil from the soybeans to transporting the finished biodiesel to market. Energy required for these processes typically comes from a petroleumbased source. Therefore 4.56 represent the units of biodiesel that are delivered to market for every 1 unit of petroleum-based fuel input. The biodiesel energy life cycle study clarifies the need to develop methods for utilizing energy dense and highly viscous neat oils that biomass feedstocks produce. The energy required for conversion to biodiesel represents a significant reduction in energy efficiency; there is also a 10% conversion loss by mass associated with the production of glycerin during the conversion process. The influx of glycerin on the market due to biodiesel production has sent its price per pound down from twenty to five cents, effectively reducing it to a waste product. One method to improve utilization of biomass-based oils while maintaining low energy production methods is simple mixing with alcohols. A recent article by Law identified butanol as the most attractive alcohol for use as an alternative fuel source. As compared to ethanol, butanol has a higher energy content and volatility. Butanol exhibits characteristics, such as octane number similar to that of gasoline and is more energy dense than ethanol and methanol [1,2]. Also, a lower vapor pressure and higher miscibility than ethanol and methanol make butanol a more desirable fuel to pair with soybean oil (SBO). Blending butanol with SBO reduces the viscosity thus enhancing atomization. The lower flash point of butanol should also improve the ignitability of SBO eliminating the need for preheating and duel tank fuel systems. Soybean oil is produced in abundance in the United States with vast amounts in storage. It is the most economical BDO for producing BDO/alcohol blends. The investigation of both butanol and soybean oil in BDO/alcohol blends is therefore warranted.

Single droplet burning is an attractive analysis method for fuel characterization. Droplet testing is a low utilization, cost effective method for determining favorable combustion characteristics, based on fuel properties, for use in spray applications. Some multicomponent fuel droplets have been reported to experience ‘‘disruptive burning", or a staged burning behavior, while others exhibit a steady burn similar to a pure fuel. Microexplosion phenomenon is well documented for single droplet combustion of fuel blends with high volatility differentials [3-7]. Recently, Botero et al. documented the same phenomenon for blends of ethanol, biodiesel and diesel [3]. The disruptive burning process is characterized by three stages. The first stage is a steady reaction of the more volatile component. A short transient period follows where the flame becomes feeble (flame shrinkage) and the dominant surface concentration transitions from the more volatile to the less volatile component. As the droplet temperature increases towards the boiling point of the less volatile component a third stage of combustion is observed which often includes a microexplosion followed by the co-burning of the remaining fuels [3-7]. Microexplosions are the boiling and exploding of the more volatile component trapped in the droplet. Botero et al. suggests that the occurrence of microexplosions increases atomization and increases burning rate. Increased burn rates are attributed to increased mixing of fuel and oxidizer. This in turn reduces sooting propensity and NOx formation [3].

To understand what governs the combustion behavior of multicomponent fuel droplets experts have investigated mixtures of a variety of different fuel types and chemical properties. Water, alcohols, n-alkanes, n-alkenes and their mixtures are the most widely studied. Volatility differentials, initial droplet size and initial concentrations determine if disruptive burning and microexplosions will occur. Avedisian studied the mechanisms of microexplosions found to occur when water was present in fuel mixtures. They determined bubble nucleation occurred in the hydrocarbon phase and boiling temperatures were insensitive to high pressure. For binary mixtures the predicted and measured limits of superheat were also found to agree over the pertinent range of pressures [8-10]. Lasheras et al. determined that a volatility difference was required to produce microexplosions in a multicomponent fuel droplet [5]. Wang and Law determined that microexplosion intensity and frequency depend on mixture composition [4]. Wang et al. also found that microexplosions are dependent on droplet generation suggesting that they can be produced by entraining air upon droplet formation [6]. Zhang and Law studied the gasification mechanism; coming to the conclusion that it is intermediate between diffusion and distillation limited. And at high pressures the gasification mechanism further tends towards distillation controlled [7]. Ikegami et al. and Xu studied droplets of heavy oil under microgravity finding four stages of burning following a distillation-like vaporization mechanism. They also investigated light cycle oil and diesel in a furnace under atmospheric pressure. They determined that the rate constant was dependent on initial droplet size [11,12]. Droplet combustion studies of biodiesel have shown typical burning behaviors of multicomponent fuels; mixtures with ethanol have documented reduction in soot production and increased burning rates [3]. Research methods of droplet combustion have included experiments conducted under reduced gravity or ‘‘microgravity" conditions to keep droplets and flames spherical and reduce the effect of convection [11,13]. Researchers have used the method of free falling droplets or suspension from fibers of small diameter(7-110 |im) [3-6,8-10]. Ikegami et al. preferred the method of suspending droplets off of a thermocouple bead [11]. However, placing droplets on the cross section of two intersecting microfibers is the proven method for producing geometrically spherical droplets [9,14]. Microfibers exhibit a low thermal conductivity reducing increased evaporation rates due to internal droplet heating [9]. The majority of research groups set combustion chamber temperatures high enough for auto-ignition [3-6]. However, hot wire [12,14] and spark ignition [9] is used in some single droplet studies for ignition. It is expected that burning to completion would always occur using the auto-ignition technique because there is energy added in the form of heat to the burning droplet throughout its lifetime even after ignition. A spark or hot wire ignition technique allows the droplet temperature at ignition to be estimated and for the post ignition combustion process to be less dependent on external surroundings. The primary interests of experts performing tests on single droplets were ignition delay time [10], burning characteristics such as soot formation

  • [6], burning rates [13], microexplosions and disruptive burning [3-5,11,14].

This paper presents combustion results obtained by igniting blends of pure refined, bleached and deodorized (RBD) soybean oil and 99% 1-butanol. The pure components were tested as well as blends of 25%, 40%, 50%, and 75% composition by volume of butanol. The mixtures are referred to as Bu100, Bu75, Bu50, Bu40, Bu25, and Bu0 where the number represents the percent concentration by volume of butanol in the mixture. The purpose of this work is to provide qualitative and quantitative information on ignition characteristics and burning behaviors of SBO/butanol blends. The experimental results were obtained using a combustion facility developed particularly for fuels of such nature as BDO/alcohol mixtures. The results are useful in predicting combustion behavior as a function of initial blend composition and droplet size. The gasification mechanism of single mixed fuel droplets during combustion is analyzed from experimental data and compared with previous research group's findings. Resultant phenomena such as microexplosions are investigated, analyzed and compared for the different mixtures. The repeatability and accuracy of certain components of the test facility are reported.

3.1. Burning behavior of pure fuels

Single droplet combustion studies determined ignition and combustion characteristics for pure butanol and SBO. Pure butanol droplets were successfully ignited at a furnace temperature of 100 °C, after 1.32 s, corresponding to a droplet ignition temperature of approximately 34.6 °C, based on the side thermocouple. Droplets did not ignite at shorter times suggesting that the flashpoint of butanol had not been reached. A pure SBO droplet was ignited using the same process for comparison. Fig. 3 shows images captured from the high-speed camera during the combustion of pure butanol (top) and pure SBO (bottom). Pure butanol droplets burned steadily exhibiting chemiluminescence too faint for the high-speed camera to capture. In contrast, as shown in Fig. 3b, SBO produces soot throughout the reaction. The bright orange flame brush indicates the soot production. A small soot cloud and soot accumulation on the fibers was also observed at the end of the burning process. Pure SBO is a multicomponent fuel and therefore does not exhibit a linear evolution. The pure SBO reactions exhibited a "puffing” type behavior as the droplet rapidly changed shapes and sizes. The normalized droplet variation with time for the pure fuels is shown in Fig. 4. The pure butanol exhibits a linear temporal change as would be expected from the d2-Law for a single component fuel. The pure SBO is not completely linear due to its multicomponent composition. After an initial heating period the boiling point of the surface components is nearly reached resulting in a trend close to that predicted by the d2-Law.

3.2. Burning behavior of blended fuel

Combustion behavior of SBO and butanol blended fuel droplets were investigated next. All mixtures tested ignited similarly to pure butanol droplets. Ignition of blended droplets of all mixtures was achieved at similar ignition times, and thus similar droplet temperatures. This is consistent with previous flashpoint measurements obtained via the ASTM D93 closed cup method. Flashpoints of mixtures were reported at the same temperature as pure butanol. Droplet size measurements throughout the combustion process were used to estimate the relative composition of droplets at time of ignition and after the first burning stage. The tendency to experience microexplosions and the intensity of the microexplosions are correlated with mixture concentrations. For all mixtures of SBO and butanol in which burning continued to completion, a microexplosion and a three staged burning process, similar to that described by the Lasheras and Wang groups, was observed [4-6,16]. The microexplosions however occurred at different times throughout the combustion process and at different intensities depending on the initial droplet concentration.

Fig. 5 shows images from the high-speed video for a Bu75 droplet burning to completion. The nonluminous flame during the first stage of combustion is evidence of steady combustion of butanol. According to current understanding the rate of mass diffusion of butanol toward the droplet surface is slower than the rate of surface regression during combustion. As a result, a SBO concentration layer develops at the droplet surface. The lower volatility of SBO causes flame shrinkage (image 3 in Fig. 5), as the surface temperature rises. The Bu75 temperature trace shown in Fig. 7 corresponds to the images shown of Bu75 burning in Fig. 5. The temperature trace can be seen to plateau during flame shrinkage. Size measurements for Bu75 at flame shrinkage show the droplet's volume to be greater than 25% its original size. During this transient period the interior of the droplet is heated past the boiling point of butanol causing nucleation within the droplet at the microfiber site. Also, the average time to a microexplosion occurrence is given in Table 1 for each fuel blend. Table 1 and the diameter evolutions suggest that for mixtures with lower butanol content a smaller amount of butanol is initially at the droplet surface leaving a relatively larger fraction present inside the droplet at microexplosion. As a result the first and second stage of the burn occur earlier. This can be observed when comparing the time period prior to flame shrinkage to the total time of the combustion process. For Bu75 this period is on average 61% of the total time from ignition to extinction. For Bu50 and Bu25 the first burning stage accounts for approximately 17% and 14% of the total burning time respectfully. This means that less fuel is burned during the first stage of combustion and that there is a higher concentration of butanol in the fuel droplet at the time of the microexplosion compared to that of a Bu75 droplet. The slope of the temperature rise for the third stage also become increasingly steep as the initial concentration of butanol is reduced. As suggested by Wang et al. this means that the burning in the third stage depends on the initial concentration of butanol and that the gasification mechanism is diffusion-like [4]. Assuming the first stage burn is governed purely by butanol reactions an estimation of how much butanol is consumed between ignition and flame shrinkage can be determined. Butanol volumes were calculated at ignition and flame shrinkage by subtracting the initial volume of SBO from the total. A plot has been prepared comparing average concentration of butanol lost (%Buloss) during the first stage over the concentration at ignition (%Buignition) for each mixture. The concentration at ignition is always slightly lower than the initial concentration due to evaporation prior to ignition. The evaporated fuel does not impact burning so the instantaneous concentration at ignition is used. Fig. 11 shows for blends with higher initial butanol concentration a higher percentage of butanol is consumed in the first stage. This leaves a relatively lower percentage of butanol remaining in the droplet at flame shrinkage. For the case of Bu40 little butanol is consumed in the first stage resulting in the highest percentage of all the mixtures remaining in the droplet at the time of flame shrinkage prior to microexplosion. This could in part explain the increased violence of microexplosions for this blend. Bu75 has the highest initial concentration of butanol and the lowest at flame shrinkage; it also experiences the weakest microexplosions of all the blends tested. There may not be a sufficient amount of butanol remaining during the third stage to cause a microexplosion. The Bu25 mixture was the closest to the Bu40 mixture as far as the amount of butanol in the droplet at flame shrinkage.

As is suggested in previous research it is suspected that during the first combustion stage for binary mixtures the droplet surface temperature is governed by the boiling point of the more volatile component. This would imply that the droplet surface temperature is the same for mixtures of all concentrations during the first stage. For Bu75 droplets the first burning stage lasts longer, thus burning more butanol before flame shrinkage. However the surface temperature of the droplet is limited by the low boiling point of butanol during this stage. For Bu50 and Bu40 blends, where flame shrinkage and internal heating occurs sooner, the surface temperature would be similar throughout the first stage but there will be higher concentrations of butanol trapped within the droplet at flame shrinkage. Therefore, mixtures where the transition stage happens sooner would be more likely to experience microexplosions and burn completely. Also, the lower initial concentration of the less volatile component (SBO) in Bu75 may cause the maximum droplet surface temperature to be depressed compared to droplets with higher initial concentrations of SBO (Bu50 and Bu40). This would reduce the tendency for microexplosions to occur. Experimental results show that when more butanol is present in the interior of the droplet at the time of the microexplosion the intensity of the microexplosions tend to increase.

  • 4. Conclusion

The combustion behavior of blended soybean oil and butanol as well as their pure components was analyzed. Ignition of all mixed droplets was achieved at similar temperatures to that of pure butanol suggesting that any amount of butanol greater than 25% by volume will lower the flashpoint. Lower flashpoints may eliminate the need for preheating or duel fuel tank systems. The combustion of soybean oil and butanol droplets experienced micro-explosive behavior due to their high volatility differential. Microexplosions have been previously analyzed and reported upon by other research groups studying single mixed fuel droplets with volatility differentials similar to soybean oil and butanol. The occurrence of microexplosions has been shown to increase burning rates and reduce sooting because it increases the mixing of fuel with surrounding oxidizing gasses. Microexplosion intensity was found to be primarily dependent on mixture composition. Near equi-volume mixtures proved to experience the most violent microexplosions. Burning rates based on the d2-law, after microexplosion, were also found to be the greatest for equal volumes of butanol and soybean oil. Therefore a range of 50-60% soybean oil by volume is predicted to be best composition for use in spray combustion applications. Initial droplet size could not be proven to have a significant effect on whether or not microexplosions would occur. Estimated concentrations at ignition and after the first burning stage were used to gain insight into burning behavior as a function of composition. Experimental results showed that less butanol was burned during the first combustion stage for droplets of near equal concentration thus leaving a higher relative concentration remaining within the droplet at the time of microexplosion. The higher concentration of butanol trapped within the droplet at flame shrinkage was likely the reason microexplosions occurred more frequently and with greater intensity. Results suggest sufficient butanol gasification is required in the first stage to achieve a temperature high enough to superheat the droplet interior while maintaining enough butanol in the droplet interior to cause a microexplosion.
